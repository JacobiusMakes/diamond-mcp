# The Hope Diamond

*History and myths*

**The Hope Diamond is a 45.52-carat deep grayish-blue diamond, colored by trace boron, on public display at the Smithsonian's National Museum of Natural History in Washington.**

The Hope Diamond is a 45.52 carat deep grayish-blue diamond, described by the Smithsonian as the largest known deep-blue diamond. It has been on public display at the Smithsonian's National Museum of Natural History in Washington since the jeweler Harry Winston donated it in 1958. Winston famously sent it to the museum by registered mail.

Its blue color comes from tiny amounts of boron in the crystal. One striking property is that after exposure to ultraviolet light the stone briefly glows, or phosphoresces, a fiery red. The Hope also has a long, documented history. Its color and size trace back to a large blue diamond that the French merchant Jean-Baptiste Tavernier brought from India and sold to King Louis XIV in 1668, a stone recut into the French crown gem known as the French Blue. That gem disappeared during the French Revolution. In 2005 the Smithsonian published research concluding that the Hope Diamond was cut from the missing French Blue.

You will often hear that the diamond is cursed. That part is a legend, a story popularized by publicity in the early twentieth century rather than a documented fact, and the museum itself treats it as folklore. What makes the stone remarkable is real enough without it: an unusually large blue diamond, a rare boron-blue color, and a paper trail running back more than three centuries.
