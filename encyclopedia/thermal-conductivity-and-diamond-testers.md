# Thermal conductivity and diamond testers

*Materials and simulants*

**A diamond tester is a handheld probe that measures how fast a stone carries heat away, exploiting the fact that diamond conducts heat better than almost any other material. Because a Lab Grown Diamond is diamond, it reads as diamond on these testers; the tools confirm the material, not its origin.**

Diamond conducts heat exceptionally well, better than any other gemstone and better than nearly any bulk material at room temperature. A classic diamond tester presses a warm metal tip against the stone and measures how quickly heat drains from the tip. Diamond pulls heat away fast and gives a positive reading, while most simulants do not.

Two things follow, and both matter.

First, a Lab Grown Diamond passes as diamond because it is diamond. It has the same crystal structure and the same thermal conductivity as a mined stone, so a thermal tester cannot tell a Lab Grown Diamond from a mined one, and it is not designed to. It answers the question is this diamond, not where did this diamond come from.

Second, moissanite is the classic exception. Silicon carbide also conducts heat well, well enough to trigger a diamond reading on an older thermal-only tester. The fix is electrical. Moissanite conducts electricity and diamond generally does not, so modern combination testers measure both thermal and electrical conductivity and separate the two reliably.

A tester is a helpful screening tool, not a full identification. It can confirm that a stone is diamond and can flag a simulant, but telling a natural diamond from a Lab Grown Diamond calls for laboratory instruments such as spectroscopy, used by grading labs like GIA and IGI. Think of the handheld tester as a quick first check and a full grading report as the complete answer.
