# depth percentage

*Diamond anatomy*

**Depth percentage is a diamond's total height (table to culet) divided by its average girdle diameter, expressed as a percentage. It tells you how deep or shallow the stone is cut relative to its width.**

Depth percentage is a single number that tells you how tall a diamond is compared with how wide it is. The formula is the total depth, measured from the table on top down to the culet at the bottom, divided by the average girdle diameter, then multiplied by one hundred.

It matters because depth controls how light travels through the stone. Cut too shallow, and light slips out through the bottom rather than reflecting back up, so the stone can look watery, glassy, or washed out. Cut too deep, and light escapes out the sides instead of the top, the center can go dark, and weight ends up buried in the height where it does not add visible spread, so the stone can face up smaller than its carat weight suggests. A depth in the favorable range keeps light bouncing back toward your eye.

What counts as favorable depends on the shape and on the grading lab's cut standards, and round brilliants are held to a tighter window than fancy shapes. Depth percentage is never read alone; it's weighed together with table percentage and the crown and pavilion angles, since one number cannot describe a whole cut. Marcel Tolkowsky's 1919 proportions were an early published attempt to pin down the depth and angles that make a round brilliant bright.

## Sources

- Marcel Tolkowsky published influential round brilliant proportions, covering depth and facet angles, in 1919. (Source: Marcel Tolkowsky, Diamond Design (1919), 1919)
