# east-west setting

*Settings and metals*

**An east-west setting turns an elongated diamond sideways so its long axis runs across the finger, from side to side, instead of up and down the finger. It is a modern, less common way to set shapes like ovals, marquises, emeralds, and pears.**

Most rings set an elongated stone lengthwise, with its long axis running along the finger toward the wrist. An east-west setting rotates that same stone a quarter turn so it lies horizontally, spanning the width of the finger. The stone and the setting are otherwise the same; only the orientation changes. It is a simple choice that gives a familiar shape a distinctly modern, relaxed look.

East-west works best with shapes that have a clear long axis: ovals, marquises, emerald cuts, pears, and other elongated cuts, including the Dutch Marquise, a trade name for an elongated hexagonal cut diamond with pointed ends and straight angular sides. Set sideways, an elongated stone can look wider on the hand and can flatter the finger differently than a lengthwise setting. A few practical points are worth knowing. A horizontal stone sits wider across the finger, so depending on the design it can sit closer to neighboring rings or catch a little more, and pointed shapes still need V-tips on their points no matter the orientation. Because the look is less traditional, it reads as a deliberate style choice, which is part of its appeal for people who want something recognizable but not standard. East-west pairs well with bezels and half bezels, which brace the long sides cleanly, and with simple solitaire bands that let the sideways orientation be the statement. It is purely an aesthetic decision, so it comes down to how the stone looks and feels on your hand.
