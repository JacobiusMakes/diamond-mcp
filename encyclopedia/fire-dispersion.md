# Fire (dispersion)

*Light and optics*

**Fire is the flashes of spectral color a diamond throws when white light splits into its component colors inside the stone. Its physical cause is dispersion: a diamond's refractive index is slightly different for each wavelength, so each color bends by a different amount.**

White light is a mix of all colors. When it enters a diamond it slows and bends, and because the diamond bends violet light a little more than red light, the colors fan out and leave the stone along slightly different paths. Your eye catches those separated colors as brief red, orange, blue, and violet flashes. That is fire.

The amount of spreading is set by a material constant called dispersion, the difference between the refractive index at a red wavelength and at a violet one. For diamond that value is about 0.044, high enough for strong, saturated flashes but not so high that the stone looks gaudy. Some imitations disperse much more: cubic zirconia is near 0.060 and moissanite near 0.104, which is why they can throw exaggerated rainbow flashes that a trained eye uses to tell them apart from diamond.

Fire depends on cut as much as on material. Facets act as tiny prisms, and their angles decide whether the separated colors travel far enough apart to be seen before they exit. Steeper crown angles and smaller tables tend to favor fire, while very flat crowns favor white brightness, so a good cut balances the two. Fire also shows best under a single bright point source, like a spotlight or the sun, rather than flat, even lighting: a diamond can look subdued under diffuse office light and show much more color under a spotlight or in sunlight.

GIA's cut grading system counts fire as one of the three parts of a round brilliant's face-up appearance, alongside brightness and scintillation. Origin does not change dispersion, so a Lab Grown Diamond has the same 0.044 value and the same fire as a mined diamond of equal cut.

## Sources

- GIA's round brilliant cut grading system (released January 2006) assesses face-up appearance through brightness, fire, and scintillation. (Source: GIA (Gemological Institute of America), Diamond Cut grading system, gia.edu / 4cs.gia.edu, 2006-01)
- Under the U.S. FTC Jewelry Guides, a Lab Grown Diamond is a real diamond, chemically and physically the same material as a mined diamond, so it has the same dispersion. (Source: U.S. Federal Trade Commission, revised Jewelry Guides, 2018)
