# portuguese cut

*Cuts and shapes*

**A Portuguese cut is a highly faceted round cut with multiple rows of triangular facets stacked above and below the girdle, giving it far more facets than a standard round brilliant and an intense, lattice-like sparkle. It is a specialty cut, more common in colored gemstones but used for diamonds as well.**

The Portuguese cut is defined by its dense, layered faceting. Where a standard round brilliant has a single main row of facets on the crown and pavilion, a Portuguese cut adds extra rows of triangular and rhombus-shaped facets, typically two or more tiers on each half of the stone. The result is a much higher facet count and a complex, starburst or lattice pattern of light. The outline is usually round, though the same faceting scheme is sometimes applied to other shapes.

Face-up, all that faceting produces intense, fine-grained scintillation: many small, busy flashes rather than a few broad ones. The extra rows scatter light in more directions, which is why the style is prized for lively sparkle, especially in transparent colored gemstones, where it is most often seen.

On durability the Portuguese cut behaves like any round: its curved outline has no points or corners to chip, so it is a secure, forgiving shape to set.

The honest practical note is that the Portuguese cut is a labor-intensive specialty. All those extra facets take more time and skill to cut, and the style is far more common in quartz, tourmaline, and other gemstones than in diamond, so as a diamond it is an uncommon, deliberate choice rather than a stock shape. If you want one in diamond, expect limited availability and to seek out a cutter or seller who offers it specifically.
