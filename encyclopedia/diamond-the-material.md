# Diamond (the material)

*Materials and simulants*

**Diamond is a crystalline form of pure carbon, with each carbon atom bonded to four others in a rigid cubic (isometric) lattice. That structure makes it the hardest known natural material, a 10 on the Mohs scale, and an exceptional conductor of heat.**

Diamond is carbon, the same element found in graphite and charcoal, but arranged very differently. In graphite the carbon atoms sit in loose sheets that slide past one another, which is why it feels slippery and marks paper. In diamond every carbon atom is locked to four neighbors in a three-dimensional tetrahedral framework, and that dense, uniform bonding is the source of nearly every property diamond is prized for.

Optically, diamond has a high refractive index of about 2.42 and strong dispersion, the splitting of white light into spectral colors, of about 0.044. A well-cut stone returns a lot of light to the eye, which reads as brightness and fire. Diamond is singly refractive, so it does not double the appearance of the facets seen through it.

In nature, diamond forms deep in the Earth's mantle under high pressure and temperature and is carried toward the surface in volcanic rock. It can also be grown in a laboratory. A Lab Grown Diamond is the same material, with the same crystal structure and the same properties. Under U.S. rules a diamond is defined by its composition, not its origin, so a Lab Grown Diamond is a diamond, not an imitation.

Diamond's hardness, a 10 on the Mohs scale, means only another diamond will readily scratch it. It is also the best thermal conductor of any gemstone, which is the principle behind diamond testers. Hardness is not the same as toughness, though. Diamond has perfect cleavage in four directions, so a sharp blow in the wrong spot can still chip it.

## Sources

- The FTC defines a diamond as a mineral consisting essentially of pure carbon crystallized in the isometric system, a definition covering both mined diamonds and Lab Grown Diamonds (the qualifier 'natural' was removed). (Source: FTC, Guides for the Jewelry, Precious Metals, and Pewter Industries (Federal Register), 2018-08-16)
- Diamond sits at 10, the top of the Mohs hardness scale. (Source: Mohs hardness scale (Friedrich Mohs), per Encyclopaedia Britannica, 1812)
