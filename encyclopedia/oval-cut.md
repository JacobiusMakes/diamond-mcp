# oval cut

*Cuts and shapes*

**An oval cut is an elongated, rounded diamond with brilliant-style faceting, essentially a round brilliant stretched along one axis. It keeps much of a round's sparkle while covering more of the finger.**

The oval is a modified brilliant: it uses the same family of triangular and kite-shaped facets as a round brilliant, arranged around an elongated closed curve with no points or corners. Length-to-width ratio is a matter of taste, but most ovals are cut somewhere around 1.3 to 1.5 to one.

Face-up, an oval reads larger than a round of the same carat weight, because its outline is stretched and spreads the same weight over a longer surface. That elongation also flatters the hand, making the finger look longer and slimmer. Because the shape has no sharp points, it is durable and easy to set, though the two gently curved ends are still the thinnest part of the stone.

The feature to watch is the bow-tie: a shadow shaped like a bow tie that stretches across the center of many elongated brilliant cuts when light passing through the middle leaks out instead of reflecting back. Almost every oval has some bow-tie, but the severity ranges from nearly invisible to a dark band that dominates the stone.

One practical point: a bow-tie does not show up in the standard grading numbers, so it is worth looking at the actual stone, or a video of it, rather than the report alone. Tilting the stone in the light shows how pronounced the bow-tie is. This is ordinary buying diligence, the sort of look you would give any stone before choosing it.
