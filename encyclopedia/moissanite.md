# Moissanite

*Materials and simulants*

**Moissanite is silicon carbide (SiC), a hard, brilliant crystalline material used both as a diamond simulant and as a gemstone in its own right. Almost all moissanite in jewelry is lab-created, because the natural mineral is vanishingly rare.**

Moissanite is not a type of diamond, and it is not a lower grade of anything. It is a separate material, silicon carbide, with its own chemistry and its own look. The French chemist Henri Moissan found small crystals in fragments of a meteorite from Canyon Diablo in Arizona in 1893, and first mistook them for diamond. In 1905, the American mineralogist George Kunz confirmed the crystals were silicon carbide and named the mineral moissanite in Moissan's honor. Moissan himself went on to receive the 1906 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for separate work, isolating the element fluorine and developing the electric furnace that carries his name.

Moissanite is very hard, about 9.25 on the Mohs scale, second among common gem materials only to diamond at 10. Optically it is livelier than diamond in one specific way: its dispersion, the fire or splitting of light into colors, is about 0.104, more than double diamond's 0.044, so larger moissanites can throw noticeably more colored flash. Moissanite is also doubly refractive, meaning it splits light into two rays. Look through the top of a stone at the back facets and you can sometimes see them appear doubled, which never happens in diamond.

Moissanite conducts heat well, well enough that it can read as diamond on an older thermal-only tester. It differs from diamond in electrical conductivity, which is how modern combination testers tell the two apart. None of this makes moissanite a fake diamond. It is an honest, durable gem with a different fingerprint, sold openly as what it is.
