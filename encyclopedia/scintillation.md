# Scintillation

*Light and optics*

**Scintillation is a diamond's sparkle: the play of light and dark flashes seen as the stone, the light, or the viewer moves. In GIA's cut model it covers both the flashes of light and the pattern of light and dark areas across the stone.**

Brilliance and fire describe light returning at a single instant. Scintillation is about change over time. As a diamond tips and turns, each facet switches between catching a light source and falling into shadow, so the stone flickers with quick flashes. Those flashes are the sparkle. The second half of scintillation is the pattern: the arrangement of bright and dark facets you see face-up when the stone is held still. Contrast between light and dark facets is not a flaw. It gives the eye distinct spots to catch as the stone moves, which makes the sparkle read as crisp rather than washed out.

Scintillation depends on the number, size, and arrangement of facets and on the quality of polish and symmetry. A standard round brilliant has 57 or 58 facets, an arrangement worked out to balance the size and spacing of the flashes. More facets, or smaller ones, change the character of the sparkle, making it finer and busier or broader and bolder.

Lighting and motion matter as much as the stone. Scintillation only shows itself with multiple light sources or a moving viewer, which is why diamonds sparkle most in a room with several small lights rather than under one flat, even source.

GIA's cut grading system, released in January 2006, treats scintillation as one of three appearance components, along with brightness and fire, in setting a round brilliant's cut grade from Excellent to Poor. Scintillation is a function of cut and material, not origin, so a well-cut Lab Grown Diamond scintillates the same as a well-cut mined diamond.
