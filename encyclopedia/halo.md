# halo

*Settings and metals*

**A halo is a ring of small diamonds set around a larger center stone, framing it with a circle of sparkle that makes the center look bigger and brighter. It is one of the most popular center-stone settings.**

A halo surrounds the center diamond with a continuous border of small accent stones, usually pave-set, so the frame reads as one bright ring of light. The main reason people choose a halo is size and sparkle for the money: the border adds visual weight, so a center stone can look noticeably larger than its carat weight alone would suggest, and the extra facets throw more light. A halo also lets a smaller or more modestly graded center stone present with more presence, and it can echo the shape of the center, such as a round halo around a round stone or a cushion-shaped halo around a cushion, or deliberately contrast it.

There are variations. A double halo adds a second ring for even more scale. A hidden halo tucks the accent stones under the center stone so the frame shows only in profile. The trade-offs are the same as for any pave work: the small stones are held by tiny beads and can occasionally need re-setting over years of wear, which is routine maintenance, and the busier design has more metal and more seams, so the quality of the setting work matters to how seamless the halo looks. Halos suit round, oval, cushion, and emerald centers especially well, and they pair naturally with pave bands. If you want the center stone's outline to stand completely on its own, a solitaire or a hidden halo is the cleaner choice.
