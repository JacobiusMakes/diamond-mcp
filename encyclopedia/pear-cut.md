# pear cut

*Cuts and shapes*

**A pear cut is a teardrop-shaped diamond with one softly rounded end and one point, faceted in the brilliant style. It reads as half oval and half marquise, and is also called a teardrop cut.**

The pear is a modified brilliant: brilliant-style triangular and kite facets fill a closed outline that is round at one end and tapers to a single point at the other. In effect it is half oval and half marquise, and it is conventionally worn with the point facing up the hand, toward the fingernail.

Face-up, the pear elongates the finger like other stretched shapes and shows a generous spread for its carat weight. The rounded end carries most of the visual weight, while the point adds length and a sense of direction. A gentle bow-tie shadow across the widest part of the stone is common, as it is in ovals and marquises.

The vulnerability is the single point. It is the thinnest, most exposed part of the stone, and while diamond is extremely hard, a sharp tip can still chip on a hard impact, so the point is protected with a prong, usually a V-shaped one that cups it.

The practical note is symmetry. The point should sit exactly in line with the high center of the rounded end, and the two shoulders, the curved sides near the wide end, should mirror each other. Because a pear is not symmetrical top to bottom, a stone that is even slightly lopsided looks off once it is set, so it is worth checking the outline against the report before choosing. Length-to-width ratio also varies, from a rounded, stubby pear to a long, slender one, and that is purely preference.
