# White sapphire

*Materials and simulants*

**White sapphire is colorless corundum, a crystalline form of aluminum oxide (Al2O3), used as a durable diamond alternative. It is the same mineral species as ruby and blue sapphire, just without the trace elements that give those stones their color.**

Sapphire is the gem name for corundum in any color except red, since red corundum is called ruby. When corundum forms with essentially no coloring impurities it is colorless, and that is white sapphire. It occurs in nature and is also commonly grown in laboratories, and both are the same material.

White sapphire's main strength as a diamond alternative is durability. At Mohs 9 it is the third-hardest common gem material, behind diamond at 10 and moissanite at about 9.25, and it holds a polish and resists scratching far better than softer simulants like cubic zirconia. That hardness, combined with a colorless body, is why it appears in engagement rings as an alternative center stone or as accent stones.

Where white sapphire differs from diamond is in the way it handles light. Its refractive index is lower, about 1.76, and its dispersion is very low, about 0.018, well under diamond's 0.044. In plain terms it has little fire and a softer, more glassy brightness rather than the sharp sparkle of a well-cut diamond. It is also doubly refractive. None of this is a defect. It is simply the honest optical signature of corundum. A white sapphire is a real gemstone, sold as corundum, and a fair seller never presents it as a diamond.
