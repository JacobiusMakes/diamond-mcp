# Refractive index

*Light and optics*

**Refractive index (n) is a number that says how much a material slows and bends light compared with a vacuum. Diamond's is about 2.417, among the highest of any common transparent material, and it is the root cause of a diamond's brightness.**

Light travels fastest in a vacuum. In any transparent material it slows down, and the ratio of the vacuum speed to the speed inside the material is the refractive index, written n. A higher n means light slows more and bends more sharply when it crosses the surface at an angle. For diamond, n is about 2.417 (measured with yellow sodium light near 589 nanometers). For comparison, window glass is near 1.5, water is 1.33, sapphire is about 1.77, and cubic zirconia is near 2.15. Moissanite is higher than diamond, near 2.65, and unlike diamond it is doubly refractive, splitting light into two rays.

A high refractive index does two things that make diamond bright. First, more light reflects at the surface, which adds surface sparkle. Second, and more important, a high n produces a small critical angle, the cutoff beyond which light striking a facet from the inside reflects completely back into the stone instead of passing through. Diamond's critical angle is only about 24.4 degrees, so light that enters is easily trapped and sent back toward the viewer. That is the physics a good cut exploits.

Refractive index is a fixed property of the material. It cannot be changed by cutting, and it is identical in mined and Lab Grown Diamonds because both are the same carbon crystal. Gemologists use it to separate diamond from look-alikes, though not with a standard refractometer: diamond's index is higher than the roughly 1.81 ceiling those instruments read, so diamond and its highest-index simulants simply read off the scale. Instead, a reflectivity meter estimates the index from how much light reflects off the stone, or a thermal or electrical probe checks a different property entirely. That is a routine, good-practice identity check, not a sign of doubt about any particular stone.
