# ring sizing and resizing

*Settings and metals*

**Ring sizing is measuring the finger to find the right band size, and resizing is a jeweler's later adjustment of an existing ring up or down to fit. Most plain metal bands resize easily, while rings with stones all the way around, or certain designs, may resize little or not at all.**

Ring size is expressed on a numbered scale, in the United States as whole and half sizes with quarter sizes available, that corresponds to the inside circumference of the band. Fingers change through the day and the year: they swell in heat and shrink in cold, so the best measurement is taken when your hands are at a normal, warm temperature, not first thing on a cold morning. Aim for a fit that is snug enough not to spin freely but loose enough to slide over the knuckle. When in doubt, a jeweler can measure precisely with a set of sizing rings.

Resizing is routine for a plain band or a ring with stones only on the top. A jeweler cuts the shank, adds or removes a small piece of metal, then solders and finishes it, usually within a size or two in either direction without trouble. Some designs resist resizing. A full eternity band or a band set with pave or channel-set stones all the way around cannot easily be cut, and tension settings and certain patterned bands are also hard to adjust, so those are best ordered in the correct size or built with a comfort solution like sizing beads or a hinged shank. Metals differ too: gold and platinum resize well, while some contemporary metals cannot be resized at all. If a ring is only slightly loose, sizing beads or a plastic guard can help without a full resize. When you buy, it is good practice to ask up front how sizable a given design is, so future adjustments are easy to plan for, since fingers do change over a lifetime.
