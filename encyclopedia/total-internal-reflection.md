# Total internal reflection

*Light and optics*

**Total internal reflection is what happens when light inside a dense material meets the boundary with a less dense material at a large enough angle from the perpendicular: instead of passing through, all of it reflects back inside. It is the mechanism that keeps light bouncing inside a diamond and returns it to the eye.**

When light inside a stone reaches a facet, it can either pass out through the surface (refract) or bounce back in (reflect). Which one happens depends on the angle. Below a certain angle, called the critical angle, light escapes. At or beyond the critical angle, none of it escapes and all of it reflects. That complete reflection is total internal reflection.

The critical angle is set by the refractive index. The higher the index, the smaller the critical angle, and the easier it is to trap light. Diamond's high index of about 2.417 gives a critical angle of roughly 24.4 degrees, measured from the line perpendicular to the facet. Because that angle is small, most light that enters a diamond strikes the pavilion facets at an angle beyond it and reflects rather than escapes, often bouncing twice off the pavilion before heading back up through the table.

This is why cut proportions decide how bright a diamond looks. The pavilion facets have to be angled so that light coming down through the crown meets them beyond the critical angle and reflects back up. If the pavilion is too shallow or too deep, light meets the facets below the critical angle, passes through, and leaks out the bottom, leaving dark spots. Total internal reflection is not automatic: the material makes it possible, but only a well-cut stone arranges its facets to use it fully.

The physics is identical for mined and Lab Grown Diamonds, since both share the same refractive index. The same principle runs fiber optics, where light is trapped inside a glass fiber and reflected along its length.
