# pavilion

*Diamond anatomy*

**The pavilion is the lower part of a diamond, the cone-shaped section below the girdle that tapers toward the culet. Its angled facets reflect light back up toward your eye.**

The pavilion is where a diamond gets most of its brightness. Its angled facets act as internal mirrors: light that comes in through the table and crown strikes the pavilion walls and is reflected back up and out toward your eye. When the pavilion angle is close to ideal, most of that light returns through the top and the stone looks bright and lively.

When the angle is off, light escapes instead of returning. A pavilion cut too shallow lets light leak out the bottom and can create a ring-shaped reflection called a fish-eye. A pavilion cut too deep sends light out the sides and leaves a dark area in the center, sometimes called a nailhead. Pavilion depth, given as a percentage of the stone's width, is the proportion that captures this, and it is read alongside the pavilion angle itself.

On a standard round brilliant the pavilion carries twenty-four facets, twenty-five if you count a culet facet at the tip. Because the pavilion sits down inside the mounting, it's largely hidden once the stone is set, so its cut and condition are best judged beforehand. Simply put, the crown lets light in and makes color, and the pavilion is the reflector that sends light back to you.
