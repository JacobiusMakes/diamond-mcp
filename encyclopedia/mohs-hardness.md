# Mohs hardness

*Materials and simulants*

**The Mohs scale is a 1-to-10 ordinal ranking of a mineral's resistance to being scratched, where a higher-numbered mineral can scratch any lower-numbered one. Diamond sits at 10, the top of the scale.**

The Mohs scale was devised in 1812 by the German mineralogist Friedrich Mohs as a simple field test. Take two minerals, try to scratch one with the other, and whichever leaves a mark is the harder. Mohs fixed ten reference minerals as benchmarks, from talc at 1 (soft enough to mark with a fingernail) up through gypsum, calcite, fluorite, apatite, feldspar, quartz at 7, topaz at 8, corundum at 9, and diamond at 10.

Two features of the scale trip people up. First, it measures only scratch resistance, not how hard a stone is to break. A mineral can be very high on Mohs and still chip. Second, the scale is ordinal, not proportional, so the steps are not evenly spaced. The jump in absolute hardness from corundum (9) to diamond (10) is far larger than the jump from quartz (7) to topaz (8). Diamond is not ten percent harder than corundum, it is dramatically harder.

For jewelry, Mohs hardness is a practical durability guide. Anything below about 7 can be dulled over time by everyday dust, which is largely quartz at hardness 7. That is why diamond (10), moissanite (about 9.25), and sapphire (9) wear so well in rings, and why softer stones and simulants show surface wear sooner. Hardness is one axis of durability. Toughness, the resistance to chipping and breaking, is a separate one.
