# girdle

*Diamond anatomy*

**The girdle is the narrow band around the widest part of a diamond, where the crown meets the pavilion. It forms the stone's outline and is where a setting grips it.**

The girdle is the dividing line of a diamond. Everything above it is the crown; everything below is the pavilion. Its outline is the shape you name the stone by, round, oval, cushion, and so on, and it is the practical place a jeweler grips the stone, whether with prongs or a surrounding bezel.

Girdle thickness is graded on a scale that runs from extremely thin to extremely thick. A girdle that is too thin can be fragile at the edge and more prone to chipping, while one that is too thick parks extra weight in the rim, where it adds to carat weight without adding much visible size. A medium girdle is usually the goal.

The surface can be finished three ways. A bruted girdle has a frosted, matte look; a polished girdle is smooth and glassy; a faceted girdle carries a ring of tiny facets. Polished and faceted girdles are common on modern stones.

The girdle also gives gemologists their main reference measurement, the average girdle diameter: the average of the stone's own diameter measurements taken at the girdle, its widest point, not the thickness of the girdle band itself. That figure is the denominator used to calculate both table percentage and depth percentage. On many diamonds the report number is laser-inscribed along the girdle, small enough to need magnification to read.
