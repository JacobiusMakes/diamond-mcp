# Inclusions in Lab Grown Diamonds

*Lab Grown Diamonds*

**Inclusions in a Lab Grown Diamond are the tiny internal features, such as metallic flecks, dark crystals, or faint growth lines, that form while the diamond grows. They're the Lab Grown Diamond counterpart to the inclusions in mined diamonds, and they're what graders read to judge clarity and to confirm how a stone was made.**

Every diamond has an interior, and most have small features inside called inclusions. Lab Grown Diamonds are no exception. What differs is the kind of inclusion, because it reflects how the stone was grown.

HPHT diamonds grow inside a molten metal, so they can carry tiny metallic flux inclusions, specks of the iron, nickel, or cobalt solvent trapped as the crystal formed. These can look like small dark or reflective flecks, and when there are enough of them, some HPHT stones will respond to a strong magnet, which is one telltale gemologists use. CVD diamonds, grown from gas, tend instead to show faint dark graphitic pinpoints or subtle growth striations, the layered banding left as the crystal built up. Under magnification these features help a lab identify a diamond as grown rather than mined, and by which method.

The same clarity scale and 10x magnification standard apply to grown and mined stones alike, though the report format can differ by lab: IGI issues full 4C reports for Lab Grown Diamonds, clarity included (IGI, 2026), while GIA now places Lab Grown Diamonds in descriptive Premium and Standard tiers rather than issuing an individual clarity grade (GIA, 2025-10-01). At the clarity grades most people buy, inclusions are microscopic and don't affect how the diamond looks to the eye or how it wears.

It's worth saying plainly: inclusions aren't flaws to be nervous about. They're the normal internal features of a real crystal, and they're part of how an independent lab verifies exactly what a stone is and how it was made. Reading them is the lab doing its job, and the report hands you the result.
