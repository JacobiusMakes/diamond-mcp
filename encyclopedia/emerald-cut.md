# emerald cut

*Cuts and shapes*

**An emerald cut is a rectangular diamond with cut (beveled) corners and step-cut faceting: long, flat, parallel facets stacked in rows like stairs, viewed through a large open table. It trades sparkle for a clean, glassy look and long flashes of light.**

The emerald cut is the classic step cut. Instead of the many small triangular facets of a brilliant, it has a smaller number of long rectangular facets running parallel to the girdle, stacked in steps on the crown and pavilion. The corners are cut off at an angle, turning the rectangle into an elongated octagon, and the table, the flat top facet, is large and open.

Face-up, an emerald cut does not sparkle the way a brilliant does. It produces broad, mirror-like flashes and a sense of depth, sometimes described as a hall-of-mirrors effect. Elongated outlines flatter the finger, and the shape reads elegant and understated rather than fiery.

Two honest consequences follow from that open, step-cut design. First, because the table is large and the facets are few and flat, you can see straight into the stone, so inclusions and body color are more visible than in a busy brilliant cut that masks them. Second, the same openness makes any lack of transparency obvious.

On durability the emerald cut is well behaved: its beveled corners remove the sharp points that make other fancy shapes fragile, so it sets securely. The practical takeaway is to favor a slightly higher clarity and color grade than you might accept in a round, and to view the stone face-up, since a step cut hides nothing. This is not about second-guessing anyone; it is simply how a transparent, open cut works.
