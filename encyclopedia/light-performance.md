# Light performance

*Light and optics*

**Light performance is an umbrella term for how well a diamond takes in light and returns it to the eye, combining brightness, fire, scintillation, and the absence of light leakage. It is assessed with ray tracing and with viewers like the ASET and the Idealscope rather than by a single universal grade.**

Light performance is the practical question behind cut: of all the light that reaches a diamond, how much comes back to your eye as brightness, colored fire, and sparkle, and how much is wasted by leaking out the bottom. A stone with strong light performance returns most of the light it gathers and spreads it evenly across the face. A weak one leaks light, so parts of it look dark, glassy, or dull.

There are two main ways the trade puts numbers to this. GIA's cut grading system, released in January 2006, assigns round brilliants a cut grade from Excellent to Poor based on proportions and on the three appearance effects: brightness, fire, and scintillation. Separately, the American Gem Society Laboratories built a cut grade directly on a ray-traced model of light performance, launched in 2005, scoring light return, leakage, dispersion, and contrast. AGS Laboratories closed at the end of 2022 and merged into GIA, and GIA now offers AGS Ideal light performance reports as a supplement.

You can see light performance without a lab. The Idealscope shows returned light as red, leakage as white, and contrast as black, so a strong stone reads mostly red with a crisp pattern. The ASET (Angular Spectrum Evaluation Tool) maps where a stone gathers its light using red, green, and blue. These are good-practice tools for checking a cut, not signs of doubt about a seller.

Light performance is a matter of cut and the diamond material, so it is identical in principle for mined and Lab Grown Diamonds. It applies most cleanly to round brilliants, where the ideal geometry is best defined.

## Sources

- GIA's round brilliant cut grading system (released January 2006) assigns a cut grade from Excellent to Poor based on proportions and on brightness, fire, and scintillation. (Source: GIA (Gemological Institute of America), Diamond Cut grading system, gia.edu / 4cs.gia.edu, 2006-01)
