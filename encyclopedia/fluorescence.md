# Fluorescence

*The 4Cs and grading*

**Fluorescence is the visible glow, most often blue, that some diamonds give off under ultraviolet light such as sunlight or a black light. Labs rate its strength from None to Very Strong, and for most diamonds it has little or no effect on how the stone looks in ordinary light.**

Roughly speaking, fluorescence happens because certain trace elements and structural features in a diamond absorb ultraviolet energy and re-emit it as visible light. The common color is blue, though other colors occur. GIA reports fluorescence on a scale of None, Faint, Medium, Strong, and Very Strong, describing the reaction under UV, not anything you see in normal indoor lighting.

The reputation of fluorescence is more dramatic than its usual effect. A GIA study published in 1997 examined how blue fluorescence changes appearance and found that, for the average observer representing the jewelry-buying public, no systematic effect on appearance was detected. In the face-up position, strongly blue fluorescent diamonds were actually perceived, if anything, to look a touch better in color. Because blue is roughly the complement of the yellow tint in lower color grades, a little blue fluorescence can subtly offset that tint in some stones.

There's one edge case worth knowing. In a small share of stones, very strong fluorescence can make a diamond look slightly hazy, milky, or oily in strong ultraviolet light. It's uncommon, but it's the reason to look at a strongly fluorescent stone yourself rather than rely on the grade alone.

Fluorescence occurs in both mined and Lab Grown Diamonds and is reported the same way for each. Treated as one modest data point rather than a headline, faint or medium fluorescence rarely changes what you see and needn't worry you.
