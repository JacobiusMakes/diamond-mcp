# culet

*Diamond anatomy*

**The culet is the point or small facet at the very bottom of a diamond, where the pavilion facets meet. On many modern stones it is a clean point, graded as none.**

The culet is the point or tiny facet at the very bottom of a diamond, where the pavilion facets come together. On many modern stones there's no facet there at all, just a clean point, which a grading report lists as a culet of none or pointed.

Historically, cutters polished a small facet across the bottom tip to guard the fragile point against chipping, and older styles such as the old mine cut and the old European cut almost always show a visible culet. Improvements in cutting and in setting mean today's round brilliants are often finished to a point instead.

When a culet facet is present, it is graded by size, on a scale that runs from very small up to extremely large. A large culet is a problem you can see: looking straight down through the table, it appears as a small open window or a gray dot in the center of the stone, which can be distracting. For that reason a small culet or none at all is generally preferred now. A pointed culet is the single most vulnerable point on a diamond to a hard, direct knock, which is exactly why cutters used to face it over; in a well-set ring worn normally that risk is small, but it is not zero. This is also the facet that explains the round brilliant's two facet counts: a pointed stone has fifty-seven facets, and one with a culet facet has fifty-eight.
