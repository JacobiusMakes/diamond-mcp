# Tolkowsky and the modern round brilliant

*History and myths*

**Marcel Tolkowsky's 1919 study "Diamond Design" set out the round brilliant proportions that still guide how the shape is cut and judged. The modern round brilliant has 57 facets, or 58 counting the culet.**

Marcel Tolkowsky belonged to a Belgian diamond-cutting family and trained as an engineer. In 1919 he published "Diamond Design," a study of how light reflects and refracts inside a diamond, and used it to propose the proportions that would return the most brightness and fire from a round stone. Those proportions became the reference point for what is often called the ideal, or American ideal, round brilliant.

The modern round brilliant has 57 facets, or 58 if you count the tiny flat facet at the bottom called the culet. Tolkowsky did not invent the round brilliant. The brilliant style had evolved over centuries of cutting; what he added was mathematics, giving cutters a target for table size, crown angle, and pavilion angle that balances brilliance and fire. More than a century later, his work is still the starting point for how a round diamond's cut is judged, and modern cut-grading systems descend from the same idea: that geometry, not luck, decides how a diamond handles light.

The practical lesson for a shopper is that cut is the quality you can see most directly. Two round diamonds of the same carat, color, and clarity can look noticeably different if one is cut to well-balanced proportions and the other is not. That is the tradition Tolkowsky started: treating the round brilliant as a solvable problem in light.
