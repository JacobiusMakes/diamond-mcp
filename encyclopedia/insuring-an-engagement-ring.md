# Insuring an Engagement Ring

*Care and buying*

**Insuring an engagement ring means buying coverage that pays to repair or replace it if it is lost, stolen, or damaged, either as an add-on to a home or renters policy or as a standalone jewelry policy. You will usually need documentation such as the grading report, the receipt, and an appraisal.**

An engagement ring is worn constantly and can be lost, stolen, or damaged, and standard home or renters insurance often limits what it will pay for jewelry. Insurance closes that gap. There are two common routes. The first is a rider, also called a floater or scheduled personal property, added to an existing homeowners or renters policy to raise the coverage on that specific item. The second is a standalone jewelry policy from a company that specializes in it. Standalone policies often cover a wider set of risks, including accidental loss, meaning the stone simply falling out and disappearing, which some riders do not.

To insure a ring you typically provide documentation of what it is and what it costs to replace: the diamond grading report, the purchase receipt, and often a separate insurance appraisal that assigns a replacement value. Read how a policy pays a claim before you buy. Replacement-value coverage pays to replace the ring with one of like kind and quality, agreed-value pays a set figure you settled on up front, and actual-cash-value coverage subtracts for wear and generally pays less.

A few practical questions to ask: Is loss covered or only theft and damage? Is there a deductible? Can you choose where the ring is repaired or replaced, or must you use the insurer's vendor? Does coverage follow you when you travel? Premiums vary with the ring's value, your location, and the coverage, so it is worth getting more than one quote. Keep your documentation and clear photos somewhere separate from the ring itself.
