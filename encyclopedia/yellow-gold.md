# yellow gold

*Settings and metals*

**Yellow gold is gold alloyed with metals such as copper and silver to make it hard enough for jewelry, with purity measured in karats out of 24. 18k gold is 75 percent gold, 14k is about 58 percent, and 24k is pure gold.**

Pure gold, which is 24 karat, is too soft to hold a diamond securely or survive daily wear, so jewelry gold is an alloy: gold mixed with other metals to add strength. Karat measures how much of the mix is pure gold, in twenty-fourths, and the math is simple and uncontested. 24k is 24 parts out of 24, or 100 percent gold. 18k is 18 parts out of 24, which is 75 percent gold and 25 percent other metals. 14k is 14 out of 24, about 58.3 percent gold. 10k is 10 out of 24, about 41.7 percent.

In yellow gold, the added metals are chiefly copper and silver, balanced to keep a warm yellow color. Higher karat means richer color and more pure gold, but also a softer, more scratch-prone metal and a higher price. Lower karat is harder and paler in color. For engagement rings, 14k and 18k are the usual choices: 14k for hardness and a friendlier price, 18k for a deeper color and higher gold content. A real advantage of yellow gold is that it never needs the replating that white gold requires, because its color runs all the way through the metal, so its warmth is permanent with normal care. It suits every diamond shape and can look especially warm against a range of skin tones. A jeweler can resize yellow gold readily, which makes it a forgiving, practical choice, and it pairs naturally with warmer-toned diamonds.
