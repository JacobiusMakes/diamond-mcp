# bow-tie effect

*Diamond anatomy*

**The bow-tie effect is a dark, bow-tie-shaped shadow across the center of many elongated diamond shapes. It is normal light behavior in these outlines, not a flaw.**

The bow-tie effect is a shadow shaped like a bow tie, or two dark triangles meeting point to point, seen across the middle of many elongated diamonds when you look at them face-up. It shows up in ovals, pears, marquises, and elongated hexagonal cuts, among others.

It's a product of geometry, not a defect. In a stretched outline, some of the pavilion facets across the center send light back at an angle that lines up with the head and body of the person looking down at the stone. Those facets end up reflecting the viewer's own shadow instead of bright light, and the result is a darkened band across the width. Because it comes from the shape itself, essentially every elongated brilliant cut shows some bow-tie. It's expected.

What varies is degree, and that's the useful thing to look at. A well-cut stone shows a faint, even bow-tie that fills in with light and mostly disappears as the diamond moves. A poorly proportioned one shows a heavy, static black band that stays dark and does not brighten with movement. So a light bow-tie is normal and often invisible in everyday wear, while a very dark one is simply worth noting when you compare stones. Round brilliants do not show a bow-tie, because their even symmetry returns light uniformly across the face.
