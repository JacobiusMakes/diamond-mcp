# Ultrasonic Cleaners (When to Be Cautious)

*Care and buying*

**An ultrasonic cleaner uses high-frequency sound waves to shake dirt off jewelry in a liquid bath. It is very effective on many plain diamond solitaires, but it can loosen stones and stress included, treated, or fracture-filled diamonds, so use it with care.**

An ultrasonic cleaner sends high-frequency sound waves through a water-and-detergent bath. Those waves create microscopic bubbles that collapse against whatever is in the tank, a process called cavitation, and that scrubbing action lifts grime from tight spaces a brush cannot reach. It is the same principle jewelers use at the counter.

For a well-set, untreated diamond solitaire, an ultrasonic is usually safe and works well. The caution comes from three situations. First, vibration can wiggle a stone loose if the prongs are already worn or the setting is not tight, so it is not a good idea to run one right before you have had the mounting checked. Second, a diamond with a significant inclusion that reaches the surface, such as a feather (a small internal fracture), can be stressed by cavitation, and in rare cases an existing feather can widen. Third, some diamonds are fracture-filled or otherwise treated, and the energy and heat can disturb that treatment.

Because Lab Grown Diamonds are grown crystals with the same physical properties as mined ones, the rules are identical for both: the concern is the inclusions and the setting, not the origin. If you are unsure whether your stone is heavily included or treated, or whether the setting is tight, skip the machine and use the warm-soapy-water method instead, or let your jeweler run it and check the prongs at the same time. When you do use an ultrasonic at home, run it briefly and do not crowd several pieces together where they can knock into each other.
