# Cut grade

*The 4Cs and grading*

**A cut grade rates how well a diamond's proportions, symmetry, and polish return light to the eye, which is what makes a diamond look bright and lively. GIA grades cut for standard round brilliant diamonds on a five-step scale from Excellent to Poor.**

Of the four C's, cut is the one most under human control and the one that most drives how a diamond actually looks. Color and clarity describe the raw crystal; cut describes the craft applied to it. A cutter chooses angles and proportions that either send light back up through the top of the stone or leak it out the bottom and sides. Get the geometry right and even a modest color or clarity grade can look stunning; get it wrong and a high-color, high-clarity stone can look dull and lifeless.

The science behind modern proportions goes back to Marcel Tolkowsky, whose 1919 analysis described the angle relationships that make a round brilliant perform well. The round brilliant has 57 or 58 facets (the 58th being a tiny flat facet at the bottom called the culet), an arrangement engineered to maximize returned light.

GIA released its cut grading system for round brilliants in 2006, assigning one of five grades: Excellent, Very Good, Good, Fair, or Poor. The grade weighs brightness, fire, and scintillation together with the stone's proportions, polish, and symmetry.

That five-step grade applies only to round brilliants. Fancy shapes such as ovals, emeralds, and the elongated hexagonal Dutch Marquise (a trade name that reads "Hexagonal Modified Brilliant" on an IGI report) don't receive a GIA cut grade, so with those you judge cut by proportions, symmetry, and how the stone looks to your eye. Lab Grown Diamonds are cut and graded by the very same standards.
