# prong setting

*Settings and metals*

**A prong setting holds a diamond with several thin metal claws that grip the stone near its girdle and lift it up so light can reach it from all sides. It is the most common way to set a center stone.**

A prong setting, also called a claw setting, uses three or more slender metal fingers that rise from the base of the setting and bend over the edge of the stone to lock it in place. The most common counts are four prongs and six prongs. Four prongs show more of the diamond and let in more light, while six prongs hold the stone more securely and make a round diamond look rounder. Because the metal touches the stone in only a few small spots, a prong setting exposes the most surface to light, which is why it is the standard choice for maximizing brilliance.

The trade-off is protection. Prongs sit above the surface of the ring, so they can snag on fabric and hair, and the tips can wear down or bend over years of daily wear. This is normal and expected, not a flaw. A jeweler can re-tip or rebuild worn prongs, and having them checked once or twice a year is good routine care. Shapes with pointed corners, such as marquise, pear, princess, heart, and the Dutch Marquise, need a special V-shaped prong to shield the point, since an exposed point is the most vulnerable part of any stone. Round, oval, and cushion diamonds are forgiving in ordinary prongs because they have no sharp corners to chip. When you choose a prong setting, weigh how much light and visibility you want against how much the setting sits up off the finger.
