# white gold and rhodium plating

*Settings and metals*

**White gold is gold alloyed with white metals such as palladium, nickel, silver, and zinc to lighten its color, then usually coated with a thin layer of rhodium for a bright, mirror-white finish. That rhodium plating wears off over time and needs to be reapplied periodically.**

White gold starts as the same gold as yellow gold, alloyed to the same karats, so 14k is 58.3 percent gold and 18k is 75 percent, but the metals mixed in are chosen to lighten the color rather than warm it. Even so, most white gold has a faint warm or grayish tint on its own, because it still contains a large share of naturally yellow gold. To get the bright, cool white that people expect, jewelers plate the finished piece with rhodium.

Rhodium is a rare, hard, brilliantly white metal in the platinum group. A micro-thin rhodium coating gives white gold its crisp shine and adds a hard protective surface. The key thing to understand, and to plan for, is that this plating is a surface layer, not the color of the metal underneath. With daily wear it gradually rubs off, most noticeably on the underside of the band and other high-contact spots, letting the slightly warmer gold beneath show through. This is completely normal and not a defect. The fix is simple: a jeweler cleans and re-plates the ring, and it looks new again. How often depends on the wearer, but roughly once a year to once every few years is typical, and it is an inexpensive routine service. If you would rather never replate, platinum or yellow gold avoids the issue, since their color is intrinsic. White gold offers a bright, modern white look and good hardness, and because it is less dense and easier to work than platinum, it usually costs less to make. Some white gold uses nickel, which can irritate nickel-sensitive skin, so palladium-based white gold or platinum is the alternative there.
