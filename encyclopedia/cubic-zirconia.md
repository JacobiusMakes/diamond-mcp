# Cubic zirconia

*Materials and simulants*

**Cubic zirconia (CZ) is a lab-made crystalline form of zirconium dioxide (ZrO2), stabilized into a cubic structure and cut as an inexpensive, colorless diamond simulant. It is a different material from diamond, softer and denser, and is not a type of diamond.**

Zirconium dioxide does not naturally hold a cubic crystal form at room temperature, so cubic zirconia has to be coaxed into one. Makers add a stabilizer such as yttrium oxide or calcium oxide and melt the mixture at extreme temperature (zirconia melts near 2,750 degrees Celsius) using a skull-melting method, in which the outer shell of the charge stays solid and acts as its own crucible. The result is a hard, clear, flawless crystal that can be cut to look convincingly like diamond.

The technique was developed by Soviet scientists at the Lebedev Physical Institute in Moscow, with the method published in 1973. Gem-quality cubic zirconia reached the jewelry market in 1977, when the Ceres Corporation began commercial production in the United States. It became one of the most widely used diamond simulants, and it remains common today.

Compared with diamond, CZ is softer, about 8 to 8.5 on the Mohs scale versus 10, so it scratches and abrades more easily over years of wear. It is much denser, so a CZ weighs noticeably more than a diamond of the same physical size. Its dispersion, or fire, is higher than diamond's, around 0.058 to 0.066, which can give it an extra-colorful flash that experienced eyes read as not diamond. It is also a poor conductor of heat, so a thermal diamond tester will not mistake it for diamond, unlike moissanite. Cubic zirconia is a legitimate, affordable stone. It simply is not diamond, and reputable sellers describe it plainly as what it is.
