# marquise cut (navette)

*Cuts and shapes*

**A marquise cut, also called a navette, is an elongated, boat-shaped diamond with two pointed ends and gently curved sides, faceted in the brilliant style. It shows one of the largest face-up spreads per carat of any shape.**

The marquise is a modified brilliant with an elongated outline that comes to a point at each end. The sides curve outward from the middle, and brilliant-style facets fill the interior, so it sparkles like a round or an oval rather than flashing like a step cut. It is also called a navette, from the French for a small boat, which its two-pointed outline resembles. Length-to-width ratio varies by cutter, but a marquise is always cut long and narrow, considerably more elongated than an oval, which is what gives it its distinctive silhouette.

Because so much weight is spread across a long, shallow outline, the marquise shows one of the largest face-up areas per carat of any cut, and it elongates the finger dramatically. Looking big for its weight is its main appeal.

The vulnerability is built into the shape. Diamond sits at the top of the Mohs scale, a 10, but hardness resists scratching, not chipping, and the two sharp points at the ends are thin and exposed. A knock in the wrong place can chip a point. For that reason marquise stones are almost always set with protective prongs, often V-shaped ones that cup and shield each tip.

The practical note is orientation and symmetry: the two points must line up exactly across the long axis, because any misalignment is obvious in a shape this linear. A bow-tie shadow across the center is also common, so it is worth viewing the actual stone rather than the report alone.
