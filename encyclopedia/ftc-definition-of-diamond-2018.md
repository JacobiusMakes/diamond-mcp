# FTC Definition of Diamond (2018)

*Lab Grown Diamonds*

**In 2018 the U.S. Federal Trade Commission revised its Jewelry Guides and removed the word "natural" from the definition of a diamond, formally recognizing that a diamond grown in a laboratory is still a diamond.**

The Federal Trade Commission's Jewelry Guides are the rules that govern how diamonds and jewelry can be described and marketed in the United States. For decades the guides defined a diamond, in effect, as a natural mineral made of carbon crystallized in the isometric system. The word "natural" carried an important implication: that only a stone taken from the earth could properly be called a diamond.

In 2018 the FTC revised the guides and removed "natural" from the definition (FTC Jewelry Guides, 2018). The reasoning was straightforward. A Lab Grown Diamond is the same material as a mined one, identical in chemistry, crystal structure, and physical and optical properties, so defining a diamond as something only the earth can make was no longer accurate. Under the revised guides, a Lab Grown Diamond is a diamond.

The FTC paired that with guidance on honesty. Sellers should clearly disclose when a stone is a Lab Grown Diamond, not mined, and terms such as "laboratory-grown," "laboratory-created," and "[manufacturer name]-created" are acceptable ways to say so (FTC Jewelry Guides, 2018). The Commission also cautioned against language designed to imply that a Lab Grown Diamond is not a real diamond (FTC Jewelry Guides, 2018).

This is why "a Lab Grown Diamond is a real diamond" is a statement of regulatory fact, not marketing. The material was always the same; in 2018 the definition was updated to match. Origin remains a genuine and disclosable difference, but it's a difference in where the stone came from, not in what it is.
