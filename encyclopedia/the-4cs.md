# The 4Cs

*The 4Cs and grading*

**The 4Cs are the four standard measures of a polished diamond's quality: carat weight, color, clarity, and cut. They're the shared vocabulary the whole diamond trade uses to describe and compare stones, popularized and standardized by GIA over the twentieth century.**

Each C answers a different question. Carat is how much the diamond weighs. Color is how little body color it has, from colorless down to a light tint. Clarity is how free it is of inclusions and blemishes under magnification. Cut is how well its proportions and finish return light. Read together, the four give a compact, comparable picture of a diamond; read alone, any single C can mislead.

The 4Cs are useful because they're shared. Before a common standard existed, a word like fine or good could mean something different depending on who used it. GIA, the nonprofit Gemological Institute of America founded by Robert M. Shipley in 1931, set out to fix that. Shipley popularized the "four Cs" mnemonic in the 1940s as a teaching device, so students could remember the factors that determine quality.

The memorable phrase became a working system in 1953, when GIA's Richard Liddicoat introduced the International Diamond Grading System, including the D-to-Z color scale and the flawless-to-included clarity scale still used worldwide. A cut grade for round brilliants came much later, released by GIA in 2006.

The 4Cs apply the same way to Lab Grown Diamonds, which are graded on the identical carat, color, clarity, and cut standards. A good habit is to weigh the four together, and against your own eye, rather than chasing one top number. The combination, not any single C, is what you actually see and wear.
